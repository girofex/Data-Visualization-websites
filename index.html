Project description

While war seems to have returned to be a constant in daily news and discussions, the public often loses sight of the real distribution of conflicts around the globe.
Through data visualization and interactive elements, we seek to provide a clearer understanding of the situation.

Conflict Hotspots: Where the World's Crises Erupt Most Often

From local disputes to large-scale wars, a global look at where political violence and unrest are most concentrated.

Around the world, not all regions experience conflict equally. According to data compiled by ACLED (Armed Conflict Location & Event Data Project), the number of recorded violent and political events varies sharply from one continent to another. Asia and Central Europe together account for more than half of the world's reported incidents, reflecting ongoing instability across these vast regions. Meanwhile, the Americas and Africa, though also affected, register fewer total events — a reminder that the geography of conflict often follows historical, political, and economic lines.

Five Ways Conflict Unfolds Across the World

From battles to protests, the forms of unrest differ — but no region is untouched.

Conflict wears many faces. In Latin America, unrest often takes the form of mass protests and civilian violence — a reflection of deep social and political divides. In Europe and Central Asia, explosions and remote attacks dominate the landscape, driven largely by ongoing war zones. Asia and the Middle East show a volatile mix: frequent riots, battles, and armed violence. Even in regions often seen as stable, like North America, the data record thousands of incidents — reminders that peace is rarely absolute. Each event type reveals not just violence, but the social tension beneath it — a map of how the world’s crises express themselves.

Faces of Conflict: Where Wars and Civilian Violence Collide

Across continents, the geography of battle shifts — but the suffering of civilians remains a grim constant.

Conflict takes many forms. In some regions, open battles dominate; in others, civilians bear the brunt of hidden wars. According to ACLED data, Europe and Central Asia record the highest number of battlefield clashes — over 72,000, largely driven by the war in Ukraine and nearby flashpoints. Meanwhile, Latin America and the Caribbean tell a different story: fewer formal battles, but an alarming 86,000+ incidents of violence against civilians, often linked to criminal organizations and social unrest. Across Africa, the toll is tragically balanced — tens of thousands of both battles and attacks on civilians — showing how state fragility fuels violence on multiple fronts. And in North America, the contrast is stark: peace on the battlefield, but still hundreds of acts of targeted violence. Each number marks not just a conflict, but a pattern — of how violence reshapes societies in ways that extend far beyond the front lines.

When Protest Speaks — and When It Shouts

Most demonstrations worldwide remain peaceful. But in some regions, the line between voice and violence is thin.

Across continents, people take to the streets to demand change — usually without bloodshed. In Europe and North America, nearly all protests stay peaceful. In Africa and Latin America, however, tension often flares: roughly one in six demonstrations turns violent. The data suggest that while protest is a universal act of expression, the conditions that shape how it unfolds are anything but equal.
